There's a version of kindness that's really just conflict avoidance wearing a friendly face. I practiced it for years. In a classroom it costs a student weeks. On a dev team it costs everyone more than that.

## The situation

Teaching beginner developers means working with people who are often frightened of being wrong. They've chosen a hard thing and they're not sure they can do it. The instinct — my instinct — was to soften every correction, find the positive angle, never say "that approach won't work" without immediately following it with five reasons they should feel good about themselves.

It felt like good pedagogy. It felt like psychological safety. What it actually was: a way of never giving anyone the direct signal they needed to actually change.

## What I believed at the time

I thought ambiguity was mercy. That if I said "this is an interesting approach, and another way to think about it might be..." I was respecting their process. I wasn't. I was making them decode my feedback instead of just receiving it. I was optimizing for the feeling of the conversation instead of its outcome.

The hard truth is that unclear feedback from a person in authority — a teacher, a tech lead, a senior dev in code review — creates more anxiety than direct feedback. Ambiguity doesn't feel safe. It feels like a trap you don't know how to avoid.

## The shift

The reframe that actually worked: feedback isn't about how it feels to give it. It's about whether the other person can act on it. If they can't, it wasn't feedback — it was performance.

On a dev team this shows up in code review, in sprint retros, in the moment someone's architecture decision is going to cause pain in six weeks and you know it. The lead — formal or not — is the person who names the thing, clearly and early, before the cost compounds.

That's not a management skill. It's a communication skill. It's something you can practice at any level of a team, without a title, starting now.
