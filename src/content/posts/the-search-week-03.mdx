Week three of the job search looked like this: two rejection emails arrived on the same afternoon. The callback arrived the next morning. I'm going to write about all three, because the pattern matters more than the outcome.

## The rejections

Both rejections were for senior frontend roles. Both came after a first-round technical screen. Neither gave specific feedback, which is both frustrating and completely expected.

What I can reconstruct from memory:

- **Company A** — I overcomplicated the component architecture question. They asked for a simple data table. I started talking about virtualization before I'd even confirmed requirements. Classic trap.
- **Company B** — The take-home was fine. The system design conversation after it wasn't. I hadn't thought through the caching layer at all, and it showed.

<Callout type="note" label="Pattern">
In both cases I defaulted to "show what I know" instead of "solve what they're asking." That's a teaching reflex. It needs to be coached out for interviews.
</Callout>

## The callback

The callback came from a team I'd described as a long-shot. Smaller company, interesting problem domain, full-stack role. I was more relaxed going into the screen because I expected less. I actually listened to what they asked instead of pattern-matching to what I'd rehearsed.

The question that went well: they asked how I'd approach debugging a production performance issue. I walked through it like I was thinking out loud — what I'd instrument first, what I'd rule out, where I'd look for unexpected bottlenecks. No canned answer. Just process.

## What I'm changing

1. **Lead with clarifying questions.** Every technical question gets at least one clarifying question before I start solving.
2. **Scope before architecture.** Define what "done" looks like before proposing how to build it.
3. **Practice thinking out loud.** Not explaining what I know — narrating what I'm doing as I do it.

<Callout type="warn" label="Honest take">
The gap between "I know how to build this" and "I can demonstrate that I know how to build this under interview conditions" is real and larger than I expected after a decade away from job searching.
</Callout>
